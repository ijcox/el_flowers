Daffodils

Daffodils are iconic spring-flowering bulbs known for their bright yellow or white trumpet-shaped blooms. They are among the earliest and most recognizable signs of spring in temperate gardens.

Description

Daffodils grow from bulbs and produce long, narrow leaves with upright flower stems. Each stem typically bears one flower, though some varieties produce clusters.

Flowers consist of a central corona (trumpet or cup) surrounded by petal-like tepals. Colors range from bright yellow to white, orange, and pale pink combinations.

Taxonomy

Daffodils belong to the genus Narcissus in the family Amaryllidaceae. The genus includes many cultivated and wild species across Europe and North Africa.

Species

Common daffodil species include:

Narcissus Pseudonarcissus – Wild daffodil
Narcissus Poeticus – Poet’s daffodil
Narcissus Tazetta – Bunch-flowered daffodil
Narcissus Jonquilla – Jonquil
Narcissus Bulbocodium – Hoop petticoat daffodil

Etymology

The genus name Narcissus comes from Greek mythology, referring to Narcissus, a youth who fell in love with his own reflection. The name “daffodil” is believed to derive from “asphodel,” a related flower.

Ecology

Daffodils are native to meadows, woodlands, and rocky hillsides in Mediterranean climates. They are early spring bloomers, providing important nectar sources for pollinators.

They are toxic to most animals, which protects them from grazing in the wild.

Diseases and pests

  • Narcissus bulb fly can damage bulbs.
  • Slugs and snails may affect young shoots.
  • Basal rot can occur in wet soils.
  • Virus infections may cause distorted growth.
  • Leaf scorch may occur in poor conditions.

Cultivation

Daffodils are widely planted in gardens, parks, and naturalized meadows. They are low-maintenance and return reliably each year.

  • Light: Full sun to partial shade.
  • Soil: Well-drained soil preferred.
  • Water: Moderate during growth; dry in dormancy.
  • Planting: Bulbs planted in autumn.
  • Maintenance: Allow foliage to die back naturally.

Daffodils in culture

Daffodils symbolize rebirth, renewal, and hope. They are strongly associated with spring festivals and are national symbols in some countries such as Wales.

They are widely used in naturalized landscapes and seasonal displays.
