Dahlia

Dahlias are striking summer and autumn flowering plants known for their highly diverse bloom forms, ranging from small pom-poms to large dinner-plate sized flowers.

Description

Dahlias grow from tuberous roots and produce bushy plants with vibrant, layered blossoms. They are widely cultivated for ornamental gardens and floral arrangements.

Flower colors include red, pink, yellow, orange, white, and multi-colored patterns depending on cultivar.

Taxonomy

Dahlias belong to the genus Dahlia in the family Asteraceae. They are native primarily to Mexico and Central America.

Species

Dahlia Pinnata – Garden Dahlia
Dahlia Coccinea – Wild Dahlia
Dahlia Imperialis – Tree Dahlia
Dahlia Merckii – Pink Wild Dahlia
Dahlia Variabilis – Common Hybrid Dahlia

Etymology

The genus Dahlia is named after Swedish botanist Anders Dahl.

Ecology

Dahlias are pollinator-friendly plants that attract bees and butterflies in late-season gardens. They thrive in warm climates and are sensitive to frost.

Diseases and pests

  • Aphids on tender shoots
  • Slugs damaging young plants
  • Powdery mildew in humid climates
  • Tuber rot in poorly drained soil

Cultivation

Dahlias are widely grown as ornamental flowers due to their dramatic bloom variety and long flowering period. They require seasonal lifting in colder climates.

  • Light: Full sun
  • Soil: Rich, well-drained soil
  • Water: Regular watering during growth
  • Planting: Tubers planted in spring after frost
  • Maintenance: Deadhead to prolong blooming

Dahlias in culture

Dahlias symbolize elegance, dignity, and inner strength, and are widely used in floral displays and ceremonies.
