Hollyhock

Hollyhocks are tall, stately flowering plants known for their towering spikes of large, colorful blooms. They are commonly associated with cottage gardens and old-fashioned landscapes.

Description

Hollyhock flowers grow along tall stems and come in shades of pink, red, yellow, white, and purple. They bloom sequentially from the bottom of the spike upward.

They are biennial or short-lived perennial plants often grown for vertical garden interest.

Taxonomy

Hollyhocks belong to the genus Alcea in the family Malvaceae. They are native to Asia and Europe.

Species

Alcea Rosa – Common Hollyhock
Alcea Ficifolia – Fig-leaved Hollyhock
Alcea Rugosa – Russian Hollyhock
Alcea Setosa – Bristly Hollyhock
Alcea Pallida – Pale Hollyhock

Etymology

The name Alcea comes from Greek origins referring to healing plants in the mallow family.

Ecology

Hollyhocks attract bees and butterflies and often grow in disturbed soils, fences, and garden edges.

Diseases and pests

  • Rust fungus affecting leaves
  • Aphids on flower spikes
  • Japanese beetle feeding damage
  • Leaf spot diseases in humid climates

Cultivation

Hollyhocks are widely grown for ornamental vertical structure in gardens and naturalized landscapes.

  • Light: Full sun
  • Soil: Well-drained, fertile soil
  • Water: Moderate
  • Planting: Seeds sown in spring or summer
  • Maintenance: Stake tall stems if needed

Hollyhock in culture

Hollyhocks symbolize ambition, fertility, and rustic charm and are strongly associated with traditional cottage gardens.
