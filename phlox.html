Phlox

Phlox are colorful flowering plants known for masses of fragrant blooms and dependable garden performance.

Description

Phlox flowers are star-shaped with five petals and grow in clusters, often in pink, purple, white, red, or blue shades. Species range from creeping ground covers to tall border perennials.

They are popular in cottage gardens and pollinator plantings.

Taxonomy

Phlox belongs to the genus Phlox in the family Polemoniaceae. Most species are native to North America.

Species

Phlox Paniculata – Garden Phlox
Phlox Subulata – Creeping Phlox
Phlox Drummondii – Annual Phlox
Phlox Divaricata – Woodland Phlox

Etymology

The name Phlox comes from Greek meaning “flame,” referring to the vivid flower colors.

Ecology

Phlox attracts butterflies, bees, and hummingbirds while thriving in sunny meadows, woodlands, or gardens depending on species.

Diseases and pests

  • Powdery mildew on foliage
  • Spider mites in dry weather
  • Leaf spot fungal diseases
  • Caterpillars feeding on leaves

Cultivation

Phlox is widely cultivated for borders, rock gardens, and seasonal color displays.

  • Light: Full sun to partial shade
  • Soil: Moist, fertile, well-drained soil
  • Water: Moderate
  • Planting: Seeds, cuttings, or divisions
  • Maintenance: Divide clumps periodically

Phlox in culture

Phlox symbolizes harmony, partnership, and united hearts.
