Daisy

Daisies are simple, cheerful flowering plants known for their white petals and yellow central discs. They are widely recognized as symbols of innocence and purity.

Description

Daisies typically form low-growing rosettes with long stems bearing single composite flowers. They bloom for extended periods in meadows, lawns, and gardens.

Their flower heads are composed of ray florets surrounding a central disc, creating their classic sun-like appearance.

Taxonomy

Daisies belong primarily to the genus Bellis in the family Asteraceae, though many similar species across related genera are also commonly referred to as daisies.

Species

Bellis Perennis – Common Daisy
Leucanthemum Vulgare – Oxeye Daisy
Argyranthemum Frutescens – Marguerite Daisy
Gerbera Jamesonii – Barberton Daisy
Leucanthemum × Superbum – Shasta Daisy

Etymology

The name “daisy” comes from the Old English phrase “day’s eye,” referring to the flower’s habit of opening in the morning.

Ecology

Daisies are important nectar sources for bees, butterflies, and other pollinators in grassland ecosystems. They thrive in open, sunny habitats and are highly resilient to grazing.

Diseases and pests

  • Aphid infestations on flower stems
  • Leaf spot fungal diseases
  • Slugs and snails feeding on young plants
  • Powdery mildew in humid conditions

Cultivation

Daisies are widely cultivated for lawns, wildflower meadows, and ornamental borders due to their simplicity and hardiness.

  • Light: Full sun to partial shade
  • Soil: Well-drained, moderately fertile soil
  • Water: Moderate; drought tolerant once established
  • Planting: Seeds or divisions in spring or autumn
  • Maintenance: Deadheading promotes continuous blooming

Daisies in culture

Daisies symbolize innocence, purity, and new beginnings. They are often associated with childhood and simple natural beauty.
