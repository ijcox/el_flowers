Geranium

Geraniums are popular flowering plants known for their vibrant blooms and aromatic foliage. They are widely used in gardens, containers, and hanging baskets.

Description

Geraniums produce clusters of five-petaled flowers in shades of red, pink, purple, and white. Their leaves are often deeply lobed and may have a distinct scent when touched.

They are hardy, adaptable plants that bloom for long periods in suitable conditions.

Taxonomy

Geraniums belong to the genus Geranium in the family Geraniaceae. They are native to temperate regions worldwide.

Species

Geranium Maculatum – Wild Geranium
Geranium Pratense – Meadow Cranesbill
Geranium Sanguineum – Bloody Cranesbill
Geranium Rozanne – Hybrid Hardy Geranium
Geranium Sylvaticum – Wood Cranesbill

Etymology

The name Geranium comes from the Greek word “geranos,” meaning crane, referring to the shape of its seed pod.

Ecology

Geraniums attract bees and other pollinators and thrive in meadows, woodlands, and garden environments.

Diseases and pests

  • Aphids on tender shoots
  • Rust fungal infections
  • Powdery mildew in humid conditions
  • Whiteflies in greenhouse settings

Cultivation

Geraniums are widely cultivated for their long blooming season and low maintenance requirements.

  • Light: Full sun to partial shade
  • Soil: Well-drained soil
  • Water: Moderate; drought tolerant once established
  • Planting: Seeds or cuttings in spring
  • Maintenance: Deadhead to encourage more blooms

Geranium in culture

Geraniums symbolize friendship, comfort, and good health and are commonly used in traditional gardens.
