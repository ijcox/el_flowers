Bleeding Hearts

Bleeding Hearts are graceful perennial flowers admired for their arching stems lined with pendant heart-shaped blossoms. Their romantic appearance and soft spring foliage make them classic shade-garden plants.

Description

Bleeding Hearts grow from fleshy roots or rhizomes and produce fern-like divided leaves. Flowers hang in rows along curved stems and are usually pink with white inner petals, though white, red, and other cultivated colors exist.

Most species bloom in spring to early summer. Some go dormant during hot summer weather, especially in warmer climates.

Taxonomy

Plants commonly called Bleeding Hearts are primarily placed in the genera Lamprocapnos and Dicentra, within the poppy family Papaveraceae.

Species

Notable species include:

Lamprocapnos Spectabilis – Classic garden bleeding heart
Dicentra Formosa – Pacific bleeding heart
Dicentra Eximia – Fringed bleeding heart
Dicentra Peregrina – Japanese alpine species
Dicentra Cucullaria – Dutchman's breeches relative
Dicentra Canadensis – Squirrel corn relative

Etymology

The common name refers to the flower’s distinctive heart shape with a droplet-like lower tip, suggesting a “bleeding” heart. Lamprocapnos means “shining smoke,” while Dicentra refers to two spurs on the flower.

Ecology

Bleeding Hearts are woodland plants found in moist forests, shaded slopes, and stream edges. They provide nectar for bees and other early season pollinators.

Native species in North America are important components of spring wildflower communities.

Diseases and pests

  • Slugs and snails may feed on tender shoots.
  • Aphids can cluster on new growth.
  • Root rot may occur in soggy soils.
  • Leaf yellowing often results from heat or drought stress.
  • Powdery mildew may occasionally appear late in season.

Cultivation

Bleeding Hearts are favored for woodland gardens, shaded borders, and cottage plantings. They pair well with hostas, ferns, and other shade perennials.

  • Light: Partial shade to light morning sun.
  • Soil: Rich, moist, well-drained soil.
  • Water: Keep evenly moist during growth.
  • Maintenance: Allow foliage to die back naturally if dormant.
  • Propagation: Division or seed.

Bleeding Hearts in culture

Bleeding Hearts symbolize love, compassion, emotional openness, and remembrance. Their unmistakable flowers have made them popular in poetry, Victorian gardens, and sentimental floral design.

They remain among the most recognizable perennial flowers for shaded landscapes.
