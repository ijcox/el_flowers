Jasmine

Jasmine is a group of fragrant flowering plants known for their small, delicate white or yellow blossoms and sweet scent.

Description

Jasmine flowers are typically star-shaped and highly aromatic, especially in the evening. They are widely used in perfumes, teas, and ornamental gardens.

Many species are climbing vines or shrubs with evergreen foliage.

Taxonomy

Jasmine belongs to the genus Jasminum in the family Oleaceae. It is native to tropical and warm temperate regions of Eurasia and Oceania.

Species

Jasminum Officinale – Common Jasmine
Jasminum Sambac – Arabian Jasmine
Jasminum Polyanthum – Pink Jasmine
Jasminum Nudiflorum – Winter Jasmine
Jasminum Grandiflorum – Spanish Jasmine

Etymology

The name Jasminum comes from the Persian word “yasmin,” meaning fragrant flower.

Ecology

Jasmine flowers attract pollinators such as bees and moths, especially those active at night.

Diseases and pests

  • Aphids on young shoots
  • Whiteflies in warm climates
  • Leaf spot fungal diseases
  • Spider mites in dry conditions

Cultivation

Jasmine is widely grown for its fragrance and ornamental value in gardens, patios, and indoor containers.

  • Light: Full sun to partial shade
  • Soil: Well-drained, fertile soil
  • Water: Moderate
  • Planting: Cuttings or nursery plants
  • Maintenance: Prune after flowering

Jasmine in culture

Jasmine symbolizes love, purity, and sensuality and is widely used in perfumes, teas, and cultural ceremonies.
