Begonias

Begonias are a large and diverse group of flowering plants admired for their colorful blooms, ornamental foliage, and adaptability to containers, gardens, and indoor culture. They are among the most popular ornamental plants worldwide.

Description

Begonias may be fibrous-rooted, rhizomatous, or tuberous depending on species. Leaves are often asymmetrical, glossy, textured, spotted, or deeply colored. Flowers are typically soft-petaled and may appear in white, pink, red, orange, or yellow shades.

Growth forms range from compact bedding plants to trailing basket types and upright cane begonias. Many species are valued as much for foliage as for flowers.

Taxonomy

Begonias belong to the genus Begonia in the family Begoniaceae. With well over a thousand species, it is one of the largest genera of flowering plants. Most species are native to tropical and subtropical regions.

Species

Notable begonia species and groups include:

Begonia Semperflorens – Wax begonia group
Begonia Rex – Rex begonia group
Begonia Grandis – Hardy begonia
Begonia Boliviensis – Hanging basket type
Begonia Maculata – Polka dot begonia
Begonia Tubhybrida – Tuberous begonia hybrids

Etymology

The genus name Begonia honors Michel Bégon, a French patron of botany and former colonial administrator in the seventeenth century.

Ecology

Wild begonias often grow in humid forests, shaded cliffs, stream banks, and tropical understories. They prefer warm temperatures and filtered light. Flowers may attract insects such as bees, flies, and small pollinators depending on species.

Some species are highly localized and occur only in specific mountain or island habitats.

Diseases and pests

  • Powdery mildew can affect leaves in stagnant air.
  • Botrytis may damage flowers and stems in damp conditions.
  • Mealybugs and scale insects may infest indoor plants.
  • Spider mites can appear in dry air.
  • Root rot occurs if soil remains waterlogged.
  • Slugs may feed on outdoor foliage.

Cultivation

Begonias are widely grown as houseplants, patio plants, bedding flowers, and conservatory specimens. They are especially valued for long flowering seasons and decorative leaves.

  • Light: Bright indirect light or partial shade.
  • Soil: Rich, airy, well-drained growing medium.
  • Water: Even moisture, allowing slight drying between waterings.
  • Humidity: Many species appreciate moderate humidity.
  • Propagation: Stem cuttings, leaf cuttings, division, or seed.

Begonias in culture

Begonias are staples of Victorian conservatories, modern container gardens, and indoor plant collections. They symbolize caution, gratitude, individuality, and deep thought in some floral traditions.

Collectors especially prize rare foliage begonias for their striking patterns and textures.
