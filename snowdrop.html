Snowdrop

Snowdrops are delicate early-blooming flowers that often appear while snow is still on the ground.

Description

Snowdrop flowers are small, nodding white blooms shaped like teardrops, often with green markings on inner petals. They emerge from slender stems with narrow leaves.

They are among the first flowers to bloom in late winter or early spring.

Taxonomy

Snowdrops belong to the genus Galanthus in the family Amaryllidaceae. They are native to Europe and the Middle East.

Species

Galanthus Nivalis – Common Snowdrop
Galanthus Elwesii – Giant Snowdrop
Galanthus Plicatus – Pleated Snowdrop

Etymology

The name Galanthus comes from Greek meaning “milk flower,” referring to its white petals.

Ecology

Snowdrops provide early nectar for pollinators such as bees emerging from winter dormancy.

Diseases and pests

  • Bulb rot in wet soil
  • Slugs and snails on young shoots
  • Narcissus fly larvae damage
  • Fungal leaf spots

Cultivation

Snowdrops are widely planted in woodland gardens and naturalized lawns.

  • Light: Partial shade to sun
  • Soil: Moist, well-drained soil
  • Water: Moderate
  • Planting: Bulbs in autumn
  • Maintenance: Allow foliage to die back naturally

Snowdrop in culture

Snowdrops symbolize hope, purity, and renewal at the end of winter.
