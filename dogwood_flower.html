Dogwood Flower

Dogwood flowers are iconic ornamental blossoms produced by trees and shrubs in the genus Cornus. They are widely admired for their elegant petal-like bracts and seasonal spring displays.

Description

What appears to be petals in dogwood flowers are actually modified bracts surrounding a small cluster of true flowers. These blooms are often white, pink, or red depending on species and cultivar.

Dogwoods are especially valued in landscaping for their spring flowering and vibrant autumn foliage.

Taxonomy

Dogwoods belong to the genus Cornus in the family Cornaceae. The genus includes both flowering trees and shrubs distributed across temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere.

Species

Cornus Florida – Flowering Dogwood
Cornus Kousa – Kousa Dogwood
Cornus Nuttallii – Pacific Dogwood
Cornus Mas – Cornelian Cherry Dogwood
Cornus Sericea – Red Osier Dogwood

Etymology

The name Cornus comes from the Latin word for “horn,” referring to the hardness of the wood.

Ecology

Dogwoods provide important ecological value, offering nectar for pollinators and berries for birds and wildlife. They are commonly found in woodland edges and understory environments.

Diseases and pests

  • Dogwood anthracnose fungal disease
  • Powdery mildew on leaves
  • Borer insects damaging stems
  • Leaf spot infections in humid climates

Cultivation

Dogwoods are widely planted as ornamental trees due to their seasonal beauty, including spring flowers, summer foliage, and fall color.

  • Light: Partial shade to full sun
  • Soil: Moist, well-drained, slightly acidic soil
  • Water: Regular watering, especially when young
  • Planting: Saplings planted in spring or fall
  • Maintenance: Prune dead or diseased branches as needed

Dogwood Flowers in culture

Dogwood flowers symbolize renewal, rebirth, and resilience and are strongly associated with springtime landscapes in North America.
